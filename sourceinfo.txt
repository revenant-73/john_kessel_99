Coaching for the 99 Percent
A simpler guide for newer volleyball coaches
Inspired by John Kessel

You do not need a perfect gym, elite athletes, or advanced systems to be a good coach.

You need to help players love the game, play the game, and want to come back.

If you keep those three things in mind, you will already be doing important work.

1. Start with love of the game

Your first job is not to create perfect players.
Your first job is to help players enjoy volleyball enough that they want to keep playing.

A good question for every coach is this:

Will these players want to come back next season?

That matters. A lot.

What this means
Help players feel successful early
Keep the game fun and active
Avoid making practice feel like punishment
Try to never be a player’s last coach
For a new coach

If players are smiling, engaged, and eager to play again, you are doing something right.

2. Players learn by doing

Players do not learn volleyball best by standing, watching, and waiting.

They learn by:

moving
touching the ball
making decisions
trying again

That is why small games and active practice matter so much.

What this means
More volleyballs usually means better learning
Less standing in line
Less watching other people practice
More actual playing

A simple truth: watching someone else do volleyball is not the same as doing volleyball.

3. Let them play

Players are called players for a reason.

They learn the game best by playing versions of the game.

That means using:

1 vs 1
2 vs 2
3 vs 3
and other small-sided games

These formats help players:

read the game
move to space
solve problems
hustle
communicate
create their own solutions

Small-sided games also help prepare them for 6 vs 6 later.

Important idea

The game teaches the game.

That does not mean coaches do nothing. It means coaches should build practice around game-like situations instead of endless disconnected drills.

4. Play over a net

Volleyball is played over a net.

So from the beginning, try to let players send the ball over something.

If you do not have a full volleyball net, use what you have:

rope
ribbon
tape
bamboo
a low barrier
even a creative substitute

The exact equipment matters less than keeping the activity recognizable as volleyball.

Also important

Make the space safe:

check for rocks
check for holes
check for slippery spots
remove dangerous objects

A safe space matters just as much as a playable setup.

5. Make the court and net fit the players

New players do not need adult conditions all the time.

A lower net and a smaller court often create better learning.

That allows players to:

rally sooner
succeed sooner
attack sooner
enjoy the game sooner
What this means
Lower the net for younger or less experienced players
Make the court smaller when needed
Divide one big court into smaller courts if possible
Use simple markers for lines if needed

You do not need perfect equipment.
You need an environment that helps players actually play.

6. Let players solve problems

The coach does not need to control every rep.

In fact, too much control often gets in the way.

Players improve when they are allowed to:

read the ball
make choices
adjust
fail
try again

That means the coach should not constantly:

toss every ball
feed every setter
hit every ball at a defender
stop play every few seconds to talk
A better role for the coach

Create the game.
Set the problem.
Watch closely.
Help when needed.

Be a guide, not the center of everything.

7. Talk less and notice more

Many coaches talk too much.

When the coach talks constantly, players stop solving and start waiting for answers.

A useful reminder:

WAIT = Why Am I Talking?

Try to do more of this
observe
listen
ask short questions
tell stories when helpful
use language that keeps players growing
Useful examples

Instead of:

“Don’t do that”
“But…”
“Try harder”

Use language like:

“What did you notice?”
“What helped that work?”
“Not yet”
“Let’s see if there’s another way”

The goal is not silence.
The goal is better guidance.

8. Coach the person, not just the player

A player is more than their serve, pass, or attack.

Get to know:

who they are
what they enjoy
why they came out for volleyball
what matters to them

You cannot turn a 12-year-old into an Olympian overnight.
But you can absolutely make them leave the sport too early.

A better standard

Do not only measure skill improvement.

Also notice:

smiles
effort
trust
confidence
connection

Know their story, not just their strengths.

9. Warm up by playing

If your practice time is limited, use it well.

Instead of spending a long time on traditional running and stretching, begin with simple volleyball activities that get players moving and thinking.

Examples:

1 vs 1
1 vs 1 plus a helper
2 vs 0
2 vs 2

These activities warm up the body and the brain at the same time.

Why this helps

Players are not just getting warm.
They are already learning.

10. Use scoring to shape learning

Scoring matters because volleyball is a scored game.

When you use scoring in practice, players care more, focus more, and compete more honestly.

You can score:

competitively
cooperatively
or both

You can also reward specific things:

a good decision
a successful third contact
communication
a new skill attempt
a smart play
The key idea

Score what you are trying to teach.

Players should care about how to win the game in front of them.

11. Choose conditions that help learning

Players learn less well when they are overly tired or overheating.

When possible, practice when the weather is cooler and the players can think, move, and focus better.

If you are in a hot climate, consider:

earlier practices
evening play
shaded play areas
simple lights for later sessions
Main point

Fatigue can hurt learning.
Good timing helps.

12. Keep more players in the game for longer

Not every good future player looks great right away.

Growth happens at different speeds.

So when possible:

do not cut too early
create more chances to play
keep kids involved
make room for development

A late developer is still a developer.

Important reminder

Biology does not care much about birthdays.
Players mature at different times.

13. Increase time on task

The more useful contacts and decisions players get, the more they can learn.

A good practice keeps players active.

What this looks like
players move quickly between reps
coaches restart play quickly
balls are returned fast
explanations stay short
game flow stays alive

If the ball is dead for too long, learning slows down.

Helpful idea

Do not make players spend half the session chasing balls or standing around.

14. Notice what players do well

Beginning coaches often give the most attention to mistakes.

That creates a problem:
players start to think the only thing that gets noticed is failure.

Instead, learn to catch players being good.

Good feedback is:
specific
timely
clear
consistent

Examples:

“That was a smart decision.”
“Nice job staying with the ball.”
“That miss was aggressive. Good intent.”
“You got the ball over and kept the play alive.”
Also teach this

There are better errors and worse errors.

For example:

hitting the ball into the net ends the attack immediately
hitting aggressively over the net may still create pressure, even if imperfect

That helps players understand that not all misses are equal.

15. Help players focus on what they can control

Players cannot control everything.

But they can learn to control:

their serve
their effort
their communication
their attitude
their conditioning
their self-talk
how they respond to mistakes

This helps players stop wasting energy on comparison and frustration.

A useful phrase

Become. Don’t compare.

Another helpful concept:
Better the ball.

That means make the next contact more helpful, more playable, and more useful for the team.

16. Keep systems simple and make them fit your players

New coaches often feel pressure to copy advanced systems.

That is usually a mistake.

Do not force complicated offensive or defensive systems onto players who are not ready for them.

Start simple.

Offense

Teach basic court numbers and help players understand where they are.

Then slowly introduce simple systems:

who sets
where the setter comes from
who attacks where
how many passers are needed
Defense

Build defense based on what your players can actually do.

Ask:

Do we have blockers who can get above the net?
Do we need more defenders around the court?
Does this hitter deserve one blocker or two?

The answer should come from your players, not from tradition.

Main point

Use systems that fit the team you have.

17. Let players referee too

Refereeing helps players understand the game better.

It teaches:

rules
responsibility
perspective
game awareness

Let players take turns with officiating tasks.

Teach the common signals and let them learn by doing.

That is usually better than only explaining rules in theory.

18. Teach simple setting options early

Players of all ages can learn to pass, set, and attack in simple ways.

You do not need to begin with advanced sets.

Start with set options players can actually succeed with.

That helps them:

attack earlier
feel more capable
understand rhythm
enjoy more real volleyball contacts

As they improve, you can introduce more setting choices and play options.

Main point

Teach what they can do now, then build from there.

19. Use simple competition formats

A lot of new coaches struggle most with organizing play.

Keep it simple.

Round robins and festival formats are often enough.

They allow:

more matches
more contacts
less confusion
more participation

Timed games can also work well, especially for beginners.

If you have a large court and a long net setup, you can often create multiple small courts and keep many players active at once.

Main point

A good format keeps people playing.
That is more important than making it look complicated or official.

Practical summary for new coaches

If you are new and feel overwhelmed, come back to this list:

Help players love the game
Let them play early and often
Use small-sided games
Play over a net
Adjust the court and net to fit the players
Talk less
Ask more
Let players solve problems
Warm up by playing
Use scoring with purpose
Keep players active
Notice what they do well
Focus on controllables
Keep systems simple
Keep more players involved longer

If you do those things, you are building a strong foundation.

Resources and equipment

This section is extra support, not the main point.

Useful resources

Look for materials on:

mini volleyball
small-sided games
sitting volleyball
game-based learning
youth development
coach education podcasts
volleyball development resources from national and international organizations
Helpful equipment

A simple coach toolkit might include:

volleyballs
portable or improvised nets
rope or ribbon
line markers
pump and needles
tape
cable ties
stakes
chalk
measuring tape
sun protection
ball barriers or simple fencing/netting

You do not need everything at once.
Start with what helps players play more.

Final thought

For most players, volleyball will not become their career.

That does not make their experience less important.

Coaching the 99 percent means giving ordinary players a real chance to experience the joy, challenge, learning, and connection of the sport.

That is meaningful work.

And if you do it well, the game grows.
